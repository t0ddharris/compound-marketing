# Messaging Scoring Rubric

Use this rubric when reviewing existing messaging, positioning, copy, or any product marketing asset. Score each dimension 1-10, provide specific evidence for the score, and offer concrete rewrites for anything below 7.

---

## Dimension 1: Clarity

**Question:** Can a buyer understand what you do and why it matters in under 10 seconds?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **1-2** | Incomprehensible. Jargon-heavy, circular logic, or no discernible meaning. Reader cannot determine what the product does. |
| **3-4** | Confusing. Reader gets a vague sense of the category but cannot articulate what the product does or who it's for. Multiple re-reads required. |
| **5-6** | Understandable but slow. Reader eventually gets it, but has to work for it. Contains unnecessary complexity, passive voice, or buried value prop. |
| **7-8** | Clear. Reader immediately understands what the product does, who it's for, and the primary benefit. Language is direct and specific. |
| **9-10** | Crystal clear and memorable. A buyer could repeat the core message to a colleague from memory. Simple, precise, impossible to misinterpret. |

**Red flags that lower clarity scores:**
- Passive voice constructions
- Sentences over 25 words
- Abstract nouns doing the work of concrete verbs
- Stacked adjectives ("comprehensive, intelligent, scalable platform")
- Category jargon without context ("next-gen AI-powered security fabric")
- Buried lede — the most important information isn't in the first sentence

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Can you state what the product does in 8 words or fewer?
- Would a non-technical executive understand this?
- Would a technical evaluator feel talked down to?

---

## Dimension 2: Differentiation

**Question:** Could a competitor say the same thing? Is what makes you unique actually expressed?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **1-2** | Completely generic. Every sentence could apply to any product in the category. Zero unique claims. |
| **3-4** | Weakly differentiated. One or two mildly specific claims buried in generic language. The competitor swap test fails everywhere. |
| **5-6** | Partially differentiated. Some unique claims exist but are diluted by generic framing, or differentiation is technical but not connected to buyer value. |
| **7-8** | Clearly differentiated. Reader can identify 2-3 things this product does that alternatives cannot. Claims are specific and verifiable. |
| **9-10** | Unmistakably differentiated. The messaging could only describe this product. Unique capabilities are front-and-center and connected to unique outcomes. Swapping in a competitor's name would make the claims false. |

**Red flags that lower differentiation scores:**
- Claims that any competitor could make ("powerful," "easy to use," "scalable")
- No mention of competitive alternatives or why this approach is different
- Differentiation is implied but never stated explicitly
- Features listed without explaining what's unique about the implementation
- "Better" framing instead of "only" framing

**The Competitor Swap Test:**
Read the messaging. Replace the product name with a competitor's name. If the message still works, differentiation score cannot exceed 5.

**Diagnostic questions:**
- What can this product do that no alternative can?
- Is that unique capability stated in the first 50 words?
- Would a buyer who's seen 3 competitor demos remember what makes this different?

## Dimension 3: Credibility

**Question:** Are claims backed by proof? Would a skeptical technical buyer accept this?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **1-2** | No proof whatsoever. All claims are self-asserted. Reads like marketing fiction. |
| **3-4** | Minimal proof. One or two vague references ("trusted by leading companies") without specifics. Claims exceed evidence. |
| **5-6** | Some proof. A few specific data points or customer names, but key claims remain unsupported. Proof doesn't match the strongest claims. |
| **7-8** | Well-supported. Most claims have corresponding proof points (metrics, named customers, technical evidence). A technical evaluator would find this credible. |
| **9-10** | Bulletproof. Every significant claim has specific, verifiable proof. Named customers with metrics. Third-party validation. Technical architecture that supports the claim. A skeptic would struggle to find unsupported assertions. |

**Proof hierarchy (strongest to weakest):**
1. Named customer results with specific metrics
2. Named customer quotes with attribution
3. Aggregate customer data ("500+ enterprises")
4. Third-party validation (analysts, awards, benchmarks)
5. Technical architecture claims (how it works)
6. Self-reported capabilities (weakest — avoid as sole proof)

**Red flags that lower credibility scores:**
- Superlatives without evidence ("the most advanced," "industry-leading")
- Vague social proof ("trusted by thousands")
- Performance claims without benchmarks or methodology
- Customer logos without context or quotes
- Roadmap features presented as current capabilities
- "Up to X%" (implies best case, not typical)

**Diagnostic questions:**
- If a prospect asked "prove it" for each claim, could you?
- Would this survive review by a technical evaluator who's read 5 competitor datasheets?
- Are proof points matched to the strongest claims, or just scattered?

---

## Dimension 4: Buyer Alignment

**Question:** Does this speak to what the target buyer actually cares about, in their language?

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **1-2** | Completely inward-facing. Written from the company's perspective about its own product. No connection to buyer problems or outcomes. |
| **3-4** | Weakly aligned. Mentions a buyer problem but quickly pivots to feature descriptions. Language is company jargon, not buyer language. |
| **5-6** | Partially aligned. Addresses buyer problems but at a generic level. Doesn't distinguish between personas or buying stages. Language is a mix of company and buyer terms. |
| **7-8** | Well-aligned. Clearly addresses a specific persona's problems in language they'd use. Connects features to outcomes the buyer measures. Appropriate for the buying stage. |
| **9-10** | Deeply aligned. Reads like something the buyer would write about their own problem. Uses exact phrases from sales calls, G2 reviews, or support tickets. Addresses both functional and emotional jobs. The buyer feels understood. |

**Alignment checkpoints:**

| Checkpoint | What to Evaluate |
|------------|-----------------|
| Problem framing | Does it describe the problem the way the buyer experiences it? |
| Language match | Are you using buyer's words or company's words? |
| Persona fit | Is this written for a specific role, or generic "decision-maker"? |
| Buying stage | Is the depth appropriate? (Awareness = problem-focused, Evaluation = proof-focused, Decision = risk-reduction-focused) |
| Outcome focus | Does it end with what the buyer gets, not what the product does? |
| Objection awareness | Does it address what might make them hesitate? |

**Red flags that lower buyer alignment scores:**
- Leading with the product instead of the problem
- Feature-first messaging (assumes buyer already knows why they should care)
- Generic audience ("businesses," "organizations," "teams")
- No mention of the buyer's current situation or alternatives
- Technical depth mismatched to the audience (too deep for EB, too shallow for technical evaluator)
- Missing emotional/social job (only addresses the functional task)

**Diagnostic questions:**
- Which specific person (role + title) would read this and think "this is for me"?
- Does this address what they're evaluated on?
- Would they forward this to their boss? To their team?

## Scoring Output Format

When scoring messaging, use this format:

```
## Messaging Review: [Asset Name]

### Scores
| Dimension | Score | Summary |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Clarity | X/10 | [One sentence justification] |
| Differentiation | X/10 | [One sentence justification] |
| Credibility | X/10 | [One sentence justification] |
| Buyer Alignment | X/10 | [One sentence justification] |
| **Overall** | **X/10** | **[Weighted average — differentiation and buyer alignment weighted 1.5x]** |

### Top Issues (Ranked by Impact)

**1. [Highest-impact issue]**
- Evidence: [Specific quote or section from the asset]
- Root cause: [Why this is weak]
- Fix: [Specific rewrite]

**2. [Second-highest issue]**
...

### What's Working
- [Specific strength with evidence]
- [Specific strength with evidence]

### Recommended Priority
[What one change would improve the overall score the most?]
```

---

## Weighted Scoring

Not all dimensions are equal. Differentiation and Buyer Alignment are weighted 1.5x because:
- Clarity and credibility can be fixed with editing
- Differentiation and buyer alignment require strategic rethinking

**Overall score formula:**
`(Clarity + Credibility + (Differentiation × 1.5) + (Buyer Alignment × 1.5)) / 5`

**Score thresholds:**
- **8-10:** Strong. Minor refinements only.
- **6-7:** Acceptable. Specific fixes needed but foundation is sound.
- **4-5:** Weak. Structural issues. Needs significant rework.
- **1-3:** Failing. Likely needs to be rewritten from scratch with proper positioning work.
